Coastline 

The coastline of Iceland is 40,652.5 miles long. What differentiates Iceland’s beaches from the rest of the world is their black sand. The sand on beaches in Iceland is made up of basalt, a type of volcanic rock. 

 The most common black sand beach among tourists traveling the Golden Circle is Reynisfjara. 

 Although beautiful, black sand beaches in Iceland can be dangerous due to their sneaker waves. Sneaker waves are “huge coastal waves that can suddenly appear in a train of smaller waves. The sneaker waves are much larger than those in between and reach way further…” 

 To protect tourists, beaches are divided into colored zones based on current conditions. If there is a yellow or red light on signs at the entrance of a beach, visitors must only enter the zone that corresponds with the light. There are no lifeguards on beaches, as rescuing those swept up by waves is too dangerous. 

 Along the coastline of Iceland, you can see the sea cliffs. These sea cliffs can be up to 1,640 feet tall. The cliffs are home to some of Iceland’s most popular birds, such as puffins, northern gannets, guillemots, and razorbills. 

 Although none of Iceland’s beaches are technically part of Iceland’s Golden Circle, I highly recommend going. Stokkseyri beach is 1 hour southeast (41 miles) of Reykjavik, and 15 minutes (10 miles) south of the Selfoss waterfall, which many people stop at during their trip. 

 When I traveled on the Golden Circle, I stopped at Reynisfjara Beach. It was one of the highlights of my trip, and I recommend that anyone going to Iceland try and fit it into their schedule, as it is occasionally missed because it is not directly on the Golden Circle path.

By Maeve Wallace (2023)
